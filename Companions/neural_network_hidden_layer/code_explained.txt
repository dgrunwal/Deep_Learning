Data Shapes

X is (2, 400): 400 example points, each with 2 features (an x and y coordinate). The points form a flower shape, colored red or blue.
Y is (1, 400): the correct color/label (0 or 1) for each of those 400 points.

Logistic regression: 47%

This is the baseline. Logistic regression can only draw a single straight line to separate the two colors. Since the flower's petals are interleaved and curved, no straight line works — it gets less than half right (worse than a coin flip), exactly as predicted.
The neural network training (the "Cost" lines)

Cost measures how wrong the network's predictions are — lower is better. The network repeats four steps (predict → measure cost → find gradients → adjust weights) 10,000 times, and you see the cost dropping:

Iteration 0: 0.69 (basically guessing — it knows nothing yet)
Iteration 1000: 0.26 (big improvement after learning the rough shape)
By iteration 9000: 0.22 (slowly fine-tuning)

The cost falls fast at first, then levels off. The tiny wobble at iteration 7000 (0.2207 vs 0.2191 before) is normal — learning isn't perfectly smooth.
Neural network: 90%

With one hidden layer of 4 neurons, the network can bend its decision boundary into a curve instead of a straight line, so it correctly classifies about 9 out of 10 points. That's the whole point of the demo: the hidden layer is what makes the curved boundary possible.
Effect of hidden layer size

This tests how many neurons the hidden layer needs:

n_h = 1 and n_h = 2 → only ~67%. Too few neurons; the boundary can't curve enough.
n_h = 4 and n_h = 5 → ~90–91%. The sweet spot — enough flexibility to trace the flower.
n_h = 20 and n_h = 50 → still ~90–91%. Many more neurons don't help here; the extra capacity is wasted on this simple problem (and can even slightly overfit).

Takeaway: a straight-line model fails on curved data, adding a hidden layer lets the network learn a curved boundary, and there's a point where adding more neurons stops helping.
